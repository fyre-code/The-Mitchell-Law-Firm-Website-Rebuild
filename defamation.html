What Is Defamation Under Florida Law?

Defamation is a false statement of fact — made to a third party, without legal privilege — that causes harm to the person or entity it's made about. Florida recognizes two forms of defamation:

  • Libel — defamatory statements made in written or published form, including online posts, articles, and social media
  • Slander — defamatory statements made verbally or in spoken form

Defamation that is considered defamatory "per se" — such as false accusations of a crime, statements that harm a person's business or profession, or statements about a communicable disease — may not require proof of specific financial harm to pursue a claim.

What Do You Need to Prove?

To establish a defamation claim in Florida, you generally must show that:

  • A false statement of fact was made
  • The statement was communicated to a third party
  • The person who made the statement acted with the appropriate level of fault
  • The statement caused harm to your reputation, business, or livelihood

Defamation law involves important distinctions — including whether you are a public or private figure, and whether the statement concerned a matter of public concern. These factors affect the standard of proof required and the damages available.

Damages in a Defamation Case

Depending on the circumstances, you may be able to recover compensation for:

  • Lost income or business opportunities
  • Damage to professional reputation
  • Emotional distress and mental anguish
  • Punitive damages in cases of intentional or malicious conduct
